What Are Our Scenarios?

The first round of model runs within the Paris Reinforce project show results from seven integrated assessment models (IAMs). With these seven models, we cover a diversity of modelling approaches and updated socioeconomic and technology assumptions.

The modelling results display mitigation efforts implied by current policies and Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) extended to 2050 and 2100 to explore how CO2 emissions from energy could look like according to current ambitions.

What do Current Policies and NDCs mean?

Our modelling efforts cover two different policy options across the globe:

  • The implementation of current policies until 2030, and the extrapolation of climate ambition in these beyond 2030
  • The implementation of NDCs on top of current policies until 2030, and the extrapolation of climate ambition in these beyond 2030

In NDC scenarios, additional mitigation efforts are assumed on top of current policies to meet NDC targets in every region in 2030 and beyond 2030. In regions where the mitigation level due to current policies surpass the pledged mitigation in the region´s NDC, there is no difference between the NDC and current policies scenario. Therefore, by construction, the NDC scenarios are at least as ambitious as the current policies scenarios.

In order to project beyond 2030, two metrics are used:

  • The equivalent carbon price: the (economy-wide) carbon price that, on its own (absent other policies), will achieve a given level of emissions reductions.
  • Emissions intensity reductions: the changes in emissions per unit GDP over time.

Each metric is applied independently to each modelled region represented by each IAM, reflecting the geographically heterogeneous nature of mitigation ambitions in the Paris Agreement.

Commonly, reference scenarios in modelling assume a ‘no policy’ baseline depicting a future where no environmental or climate policies are implemented. However, it is becoming evident that climate policies are indeed having an impact, in line with the Paris Agreement and ratcheting ambitions. With these forward projections of mitigation efforts beyond 2030, we generate new reference baseline scenarios to 2050 and 2100. These new baseline scenarios reflect where emissions are headed if current efforts are continued - filling a critical gap in the scenario space that is currently underexplored.

What do the min-max of each scenario say across the diversity of models in terms of emissions?

The overall range of global fossil energy CO2 emissions in our scenarios show the following:

In 2030: 31-38GtCO2 (32-38 GtCO2 150 for Current Policies scenarios and 31-35 GtCO2 for NDC scenarios)

In 2050: 20-41GtCO2 (23-151 41GtCO2 for Current Policies scenarios and 20-38GtCO2 for NDC scenarios)

Implications for reinforcing targets towards the PA 2030 and goals beyond?

Our results show variation in emissions trajectories due to model differences.

Therefore, exploratory pathways for mitigation efforts should always be based on diverse sets of modelling approaches.

  • Our scenarios show a level of ambition that is significantly higher than what ‘No Policy’ baselines indicate
  • As also indicated by the NDC synthesis report published in Feb 2021, all our scenarios still show a level of ambition that is insufficient to limit global warming to “well below” 2°C.
  • Current policies still lag behind NDCs in many regions.

To achieve the Paris Agreement goal, global ambitions will have to be strengthened.

What role does technology play in this?

While the energy system is decarbonized, the use of Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) contributes significantly to the reduction in energy CO2 emissions in all models that include CCS.
